# Who's Going

One rider from this household joins a **guided tour** — this is not a self-organized trip. The operator (**[Silk Off Road Tours](https://www.silkoffroadtours.com/motorcycle-tours-kazakhstan-kyrgyzstan)**) supplies the bike, the ride leader, and a support vehicle. This departure is a **custom extended loop edition** (16 days, Almaty → Almaty — arrival + a 14-day tour body + departure, the same 14+2 shape as the Morocco tour) of the operator's published 10-day tour — group size and single-supplement terms to confirm with the operator.

## Who can join

The rest of the group is **not fixed yet** — which is exactly why the plan is built on the [anchor model](03-anchors-and-options.md): five pre-booked, non-negotiable anchor nights, with easier/harder **route options** between them so the connections scale to whoever signs on. The **minimum bar stays the operator's requirements** (below: international license + IDP, 21+, 3+ years riding with solid off-road skills, physical endurance) — the options don't lower the floor, they scale the ceiling. The **Day-2 training day is where the options get chosen**: the ride leader watches the group through the drills and the foothill loop, and that verdict picks the glacier road vs. the coast road, Tosor vs. the Kochkor split.

## Ruslan — the rider

- **Experience:** seasoned ADV rider. Daily bike at home is a **BMW R1300GS**; previously completed a **guided ADV training tour in Morocco** (group format, mixed tarmac/gravel/desert pistes — the same "anchor-point adventure" style as this trip).
- **On this trip:** rides the operator's **rental Suzuki DR650SE** (43 hp, 865–885 mm seat, ~160 kg wet) — a deliberate step down in weight and electronics from the GS, and a step up in terrain: real gravel passes, desert pistes, and a 4,021 m summit.
- **Why this tour:** the Morocco trip proved the format works for him; Kazakhstan/Kyrgyzstan is the graduation ride — longer off-road days, real altitude, remote nights (three yurt-camp nights, a cabin camp), and two border crossings by bike.
- **Role in the group:** a mid-pack rider, not the limiter. Comfortable standing on the pegs on gravel, fine with 250–325 km days, no sag-wagon needs expected. The luggage rides in the support truck, so the DR650 stays light.

## Day 2 — the training day (Morocco-style)

Before any big leg, **Day 2 — the first day of the 14-day tour body — is a dedicated skills-and-shakedown day** out of Almaty — the same warm-up formula that made the Morocco tour work:

- **Bike setup:** fit the DR650 to the rider — lever and bar position, tire pressures for mixed surfaces, suspension sag, and a full walk-around so Day-3 surprises happen in the hotel car park, not on the steppe.
- **Off-road stance:** standing position, weighting the pegs, looking through the turn — re-grooving the Morocco habits on an unfamiliar, lighter bike.
- **Gravel braking:** front/rear balance on loose surface, threshold practice at low speed, engine-braking on descents.
- **Sand basics:** momentum, loose grip, paddling and recovery — rehearsal for the Singing Dune pistes on Day 4.
- Then an **easy ~120 km foothill/steppe loop** to bed it all in and calibrate pace with the ride leader.

The point: **dial in the bike and the reflexes while the stakes are low**, so the hard block is ridden on a machine that already feels like his.

## The rest-day logic (Day 8)

The old 10-day route had no rest day — recovery was just the shorter legs. The loop edition fixes that: **Day 8 is a full no-riding day at the Issyk-Kul south-shore yurt camp**, placed deliberately:

- It lands **immediately after the 4,021 m Suek day** (Day 7) — the single hardest leg — so the body recovers before four more mountain days.
- It sits at lake altitude (~1,600 m), aiding acclimatization between the 4,000 m crossing and the 3,000 m nights at Song-Kol.
- It's a maintenance window: laundry, bike checks (chain, spokes, levers after the gravel), and a long look at the lake — Skazka canyon is a short walk-friendly outing.
- The two-night stay at **Karakol (D5–6)** serves the same purpose earlier in lighter form: a riding day, but no camp-moving, no packing. (Altyn-Emel is one night — the Singing Dune and Aktau are ridden en route to Chundzha on Day 4.)

## The operator's requirements (and what they actually mean)

The operator's published requirements, verbatim in spirit:

| Requirement | In practice |
|---|---|
| **International motorcycle license + IDP** | Home license with motorcycle endorsement **plus** an International Driving Permit (AAA, ~15 minutes, bring two passport photos). Carried every riding day; police checks on KZ/KG highways are routine. |
| **Age 21+** | Met. |
| **3+ years riding, solid off-road skills** | ~40% of the route is gravel/dirt: washboard, sand patches near the Singing Dune, rocky descents off Song-Kol. You must be able to pick your own lines, ride standing, and handle a 160 kg bike in loose surface — the Day-2 session is a refresher and shakedown, not a beginner school; the ride leader guides, he doesn't teach basics. |
| **Physical endurance** | Thirteen riding days incl. the training day (110–325 km each, much of it off-pavement) over sixteen, with one rest day. Fitness matters more than speed. |

## The conditions the requirements are really about

- **Altitude.** Three days go above 3,000 m and Day 7 crests **Suek Pass at 4,021 m**; two nights sleep at ~3,000 m (Song-Kol is at 3,016 m). Expect shortness of breath on foot, weaker engine response, and cold — ride hydrated, skip alcohol the night before the big passes.
- **Heat *and* cold in the same trip.** The Altyn-Emel steppe and Chundzha desert days can hit 30 °C+; the glacier passes and yurt-camp nights can drop below freezing. Layering is the whole packing strategy (see [02-getting-started.md](02-getting-started.md)).
- **Off-road days back-to-back.** **Days 7–12 are the hard block:** the 4,021 m glacier day, then (after the Day-8 rest) Song-Kol's dirt approaches and the rocky descent to Suusamyr. The rest day splits the block after its hardest opener.
- **Remoteness.** No phone coverage at Song-Kol, on the high passes, and patchy around Saty/Kolsai; medical help is hours away. This is why personal **medical + evacuation insurance that explicitly covers motorcycling** is non-negotiable (it is *excluded* from the tour price).

## What the guided format provides

- **Ride leader** sets pace and lines; nobody navigates solo.
- **Support vehicle** carries all luggage, spares and water, and can carry a rider (or bike) that has a bad day.
- **Fixed nightly anchors** — hotels, guest houses, a hot-springs resort, two yurt camps (three yurt nights), a cabin camp — so the adventurous part is the riding, not the logistics.
- **Both border runs handled** — the operator does the bike paperwork at Kegen/Karkara in both directions (Day 5 southbound, Day 14 northbound); the rider only queues with his passport.
