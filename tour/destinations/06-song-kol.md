# Song-Kol Lake

> An alpine lake at 3,016 m ringed by summer pastures and nomad yurts — horses to the horizon, kumis in the bowl, and one of the darkest night skies you will ever sleep under.

- **Region:** Naryn Province, Kyrgyzstan · 3,016 m
- **Itinerary:** Day 10 · 1 night (nomad yurt camp; bad-weather fallback: Kyzart village eco-hotel)
- **Stop type:** stop
- **Ride to here:** 130 km (Kochkor via the 33 Parrots Pass, 3,133 m, Day 10)

## About
**Song-Kol** (Сон-Көл) is Kyrgyzstan's highest large lake — 29 km by 18 km of cold, glass-clear water at **3,016 m**, set in a treeless bowl of grass so wide the horizon curves. There are no towns, no power lines and effectively no buildings: in summer the shore belongs to the **jailoo**, the high pastures, where nomad families pitch their yurts from June to September and graze thousands of horses, sheep and yaks exactly as their great-grandparents did. Staying the night in one of their camps — not looking at nomad life, but sleeping inside it — is for many riders the single best night of the tour.

The way in is a short day with a big centrepiece: the **"33 Parrots" Pass (3,133 m)**, a stack of tight hairpin switchbacks winding up the mountain wall (the nickname comes from a beloved Soviet cartoon — count the bends). Over the top, the road turns to dirt across open pasture and the lake appears, impossibly blue against the yellow-green grass. **It is cold here even in July** — expect single digits after sunset, possible frost, and weather that can close the pass; if it does, the group falls back to an **eco-hotel in Kyzart village** below.

## Things to see & do
- **The 33 Parrots Pass (3,133 m)** — The day's riding highlight: a wall of numbered hairpins climbing out of the Kochkor valley, gravel in the upper reaches, with the whole valley unrolling behind you (**take it at your own pace behind the leader**).
- **Life on the jailoo** — Walk between the yurts: mares being milked at dusk, foals tethered in lines, kids on horseback herding sheep barefoot. Guests are welcome to help with the animals — just ask your hosts.
- **Horses everywhere** — Song-Kol in July is one huge horse pasture; most camps will happily saddle one for a short ride along the shore (**agree the price first, typically $10–15/hour**).
- **The night sky** — At 3,016 m, with zero light pollution, the Milky Way is bright enough to cast shadows; step out of the yurt around midnight (**and bring every layer you own**).
- **Birdlife on the lake** — Song-Kol is a state reserve and Ramsar site: mountain geese, gulls and demoiselle cranes work the shallows at dawn.

## What to eat
- **Kumis** — Fermented mare's milk, mildly fizzy, sour and faintly alcoholic — the drink of the jailoo, offered to every guest; trying it is the toll for the view (a small bowl is polite, nobody minds if you don't finish).
- **Boorsok** — Puffy squares of fried dough, tipped in a heap on the dastorkon and eaten with butter, thick cream (**kaymak**) and apricot jam.
- **Herders' dinner in the yurt** — Whatever the family cooks: usually **shorpo** mutton soup, fresh noodles or **plov**, bread and tea; hot, simple and exactly right at 3,000 m. Dinners are at your own expense — cash only, there is nothing to buy at the lake, so carry snacks from Kochkor.
