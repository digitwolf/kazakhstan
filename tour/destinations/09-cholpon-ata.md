# Cholpon-Ata

> Issyk-Kul's sunny-side resort town — an easy asphalt day out of Bishkek through the Boom gorge, an evening swim off the north-shore beach, and a field of 5,000 boulders carved by Bronze Age and Saka hands.

- **Region:** Issyk-Kul north shore, Kyrgyzstan
- **Itinerary:** Day 13 · 1 night (hotel)
- **Stop type:** stop
- **Ride to here:** 261 km (Bishkek through the Boom gorge to the north shore, Day 13)

## About
**Cholpon-Ata** is the capital of Issyk-Kul's holiday coast — the north shore, the sunny, sandy, resort-lined side of the lake that the tour skipped on the way south. Where the south shore was yurts and pebbles, the north is beaches, promenades, shashlik smoke and Soviet-era sanatoriums under the snow wall of the **Kungey Alatau**; across the water, the **Terskey Alatau** you crossed on Day 7 floats on the horizon. The town is also the lake's cultural showpiece: it hosts the hippodrome built for the **World Nomad Games** and, just uphill from the resorts, one of Central Asia's great open-air archaeological sites.

That site is the **Cholpon-Ata petroglyphs** — a "stone garden" of some **5,000 glacial boulders spread over 42 hectares** between the town and the mountains, carved with ibex, deer, horsemen and hunting scenes. The oldest images date to the end of the **Bronze Age (around 1500 BC)**; most were cut by the **Saka-Usun tribes (8th century BC – 1st century AD)**, who held this slope sacred and used it for sun-god rituals, and the youngest belong to the Turkic era (5th–10th centuries). The ride in is the loop's recovery day: 261 km of honest asphalt east from Bishkek, funnelling through the **Boom gorge** — the red-rock canyon of the Chüy river that is the only natural gate between the capital's valley and the Issyk-Kul basin — before the lake opens out blue on your right for the last 100 km along the shore.

## Things to see & do
- **The open-air petroglyph field ("stone garden")** — Wander marked paths through 42 hectares of carved boulders with the lake below and the Kungey Alatau behind — Bronze Age to Saka-Usun to Turkic images, plus balbal stone figures (**go in the late-afternoon side light, when the carvings jump out of the rock; entry is a small cash fee**).
- **Beach evening on the north shore** — The classic Cholpon-Ata program: a swim off the sandy town beach, then shashlik and a cold drink on the promenade while the sun drops behind the mountains across the lake.
- **Ruh Ordo cultural complex** — A lakefront "spiritual city" named after the writer **Chingiz Aitmatov**: five white chapels for five world faiths, sculpture gardens and a long shoreline walk — kitschy to some, photogenic to all (**an easy sunset stroll from the centre**).
- **The Boom gorge (en route)** — Day 13's riding set-piece: the Chüy river's red-walled canyon carrying the road and railway side by side out of the Chüy valley — after four days of gravel passes, flowing tarmac feels like a gift (**watch the gusty wind where the gorge opens toward Balykchy**).
- **World Nomad Games hippodrome** — The giant horse-games arena that hosted the first three **World Nomad Games** (2014–2018); worth a look from the gate even outside event days.

## What to eat
- **Lakeside fish** — The north-shore staple: whole fried or hot-smoked Issyk-Kul fish sold along the promenade and in shore cafés — with bread, herbs and a squeeze of lemon it's the best simple dinner on the lake.
- **Shashlik on the promenade** — Skewers of lamb and beef grilling from late afternoon all along the beachfront strip; eaten standing up with raw onion and vinegar, washed down with kompot or a local beer.
- **Resort-town classics** — Proper **lagman**, **manty** and plov in the cafés on the main road, plus roadside apricots, cherries and **kurut** from stands on the shore highway — dinners are at your own expense, and unlike the south shore, cards work in the bigger places (still carry som).
