# Karakol

> Charyn Canyon at sunrise, a quiet mountain border into Kyrgyzstan, then two nights in the adventure capital — with a day loop to the Seven Bulls red cliffs and the hot springs of Altyn-Arashan in between.

- **Region:** Karakol, Issyk-Kul Region, Kyrgyzstan
- **Itinerary:** Day 5–6 (2 nights — Day 6 is the Jeti-Oguz & Altyn-Arashan day loop, ~110 km)
- **Stop type:** stay
- **Ride to here:** 280 km (Chundzha → Karakol: Charyn Canyon, the Kegen–Karkara border crossing into Kyrgyzstan, and the flanks of the Ketmen range; Day 5)

## About
Day 5 is the tour's great transition: it starts in Kazakh desert and ends in a Kyrgyz mountain town. First, less than an hour from Chundzha, comes **Charyn Canyon** — then the route climbs southwest along the flanks of the **Ketmen range** to the little steppe town of **Kegen** and the **Karkara valley border crossing** into Kyrgyzstan: a quiet, summer-friendly post where a handful of riders and horsemen outnumber the trucks. On the far side, the road sweeps down green alpine meadows toward Lake Issyk-Kul and into **Karakol**, 280 km from the morning's hot springs.

Karakol is Kyrgyzstan's fourth-largest town and its adventure capital, founded in 1869 as a Russian garrison outpost near the eastern tip of Issyk-Kul. Its streets still carry the layers of everyone who settled here: tsarist officers, Dungan refugees from China, and the explorer **Nikolai Przhevalsky**, who died here in 1888 and gave the town its Soviet-era name, Przhevalsk. The loop stays **two nights**: Day 5 evening for the landmarks — the **Holy Trinity Russian Orthodox Church** and the **Dungan Mosque** — and dinner in a town that takes its food remarkably seriously, then **Day 6 riding light on a ~110 km day loop** into the Terskey Alatau: the red **Jeti-Oguz "Seven Bulls" cliffs** in the morning and the rough track up to the **Altyn-Arashan hot springs (~2,600 m)** in the afternoon, back to the same hotel with the luggage never having moved.

## Things to see & do
- **Charyn Canyon — the Valley of Castles** — The day's headline, en route: a red sandstone gorge where 12-million-year-old rock has eroded into towers, arches, and fortress walls up to ~100 m high along a 2-km valley (the canyon runs 150 m+ deep elsewhere). Walk the valley floor to the Charyn River viewpoint; go early for cool air and empty trails. Entry is a small per-person park fee — the operator handles it.
- **The Kegen–Karkara border crossing** — Kazakhstan → Kyrgyzstan the scenic way: a small seasonal post in the high Karkara valley, all green pasture and yurts. Passports, bike documents, twenty minutes of stamps — and the tour is in country number two.
- **Karkara valley riding** — The descent from the border toward Issyk-Kul is the day's riding reward: open alpine valley, snow peaks of the Terskey Alatau ahead, and barely any traffic.
- **Jeti-Oguz — the Seven Bulls (Day-6 morning)** — About 28 km southwest of town, a wall of sheer **red sandstone cliffs** rises out of the spruce forest: seven ribs of rock said to be seven petrified bulls, with the split **"Broken Heart" rock** guarding the gorge entrance. Paved road to the village, then easy gravel up the gorge past the old Soviet spa to the Kok-Jaiyk "Valley of Flowers" meadows — photo stop upon photo stop.
- **Altyn-Arashan valley track (Day-6 afternoon)** — The day's riding main course: from Ak-Suu village (Teploklyuchenka) a genuinely **rough 4x4/moto track climbs ~14 km** up the Arashan river gorge — rocks, ruts, stream splashes, the stuff the DR650 was rented for — to a green valley at **~2,600 m** where **hot springs** (hydrogen-sulfide pools to ~41 °C) steam beside the river below the snow pyramid of Palatka peak. Soak, drink tea at a yurt camp, ride back down. Weather-dependent; the support truck waits at the valley mouth.
- **Holy Trinity Russian Orthodox Church** — Karakol's wooden cathedral, rebuilt in 1895 after an earthquake destroyed the original; green domes and golden crosses over log walls. Evening light is perfect for it.
- **Dungan Mosque** — Commissioned in 1904 and built over six years by Beijing architect Chou Seu and a team of master carvers — in Chinese pagoda style, held together by joinery on 42 wooden pillars and famously **built without a single nail**. Still an active mosque; visitors welcome outside prayer times.
- **Przhevalsky legacy** — The explorer Nikolai Przhevalsky (of Przewalski's-horse fame) made Karakol his base and is buried above the lake shore nearby; his memorial and museum at Pristan-Przhevalsk make a short detour if the group arrives with daylight to spare.

## What to eat
- **Ashlan-fuu** — Karakol's own dish and a point of civic pride: a cold, spicy Dungan bowl of noodles and wobbly starch jelly in a vinegar-chili broth, sold for pennies at the cafés around the bazaar (there's practically an ashlan-fuu alley). Mandatory, ideally with a fried piroshki on the side.
- **Dungan family dinner** — Karakol's signature food experience: a multi-course meal of Dungan (Chinese-Muslim) home dishes hosted by a local family — book through Destination Karakol or your guide.
- **Lagman, Kyrgyz edition** — Compare notes with last night's Uighur version — fried lagman (boso lagman) is the local move.
- **Kyrgyz staples** — Manty, samsy from tandyr ovens, honey and apricots from Issyk-Kul orchards, and endless green tea.
