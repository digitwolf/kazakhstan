## The road as teacher — a skill curriculum, in order

The genius of this loop for a new rider is that the **difficulty rises gently and deliberately**,
each day teaching one new thing before the next:

1. **Day 1 — first mile confidence.** The **ferry roll-on** is a calm "you can do this" first; the **Hood Canal shore (WA-106)** is the gentlest possible touring road — wide, slow, flowing — to settle into riding loaded, all day, in a group. *Lesson: rhythm, smoothness, trusting the bike.*
2. **Day 2 — wind & a big structure.** Flat coast plus the **4-mile Astoria Bridge** in coastal wind. *Lesson: holding a calm line through crosswind and a long exposed crossing.*
3. **Day 3 — the long, varied day.** Many short scenic legs, summer traffic, RVs, the twistier **Three Capes Loop**. *Lesson: patience, no risky passing, managing a full day of stop-start touring and tighter corners.*
4. **Day 4 — rest & reset.** Bikes parked. *Lesson: recovery is part of touring; come back fresh.*
5. **Day 5 — the long transfer.** The trip's longest day (~217 mi) on the flowing **Alsea River road** and valley two-lanes. *Lesson: pacing yourself over distance, fuel discipline on the small tank, steady all-day endurance.*
6. **Day 6 — real mountain sweepers.** **Spirit Lake Hwy (SR-504)** — smooth, modern, climbing sweepers with elevation. *Lesson: cornering with confidence on an open mountain road.*
7. **Day 7 — graduation.** **Stevens Canyon → Paradise**, then the **Chinook Pass (SR-410) switchbacks** at 5,430 ft, then the long descent home. *Lesson: sustained switchbacks, altitude, descending control — and the pride of finishing your first tour.*

**The point:** by Day 7 she has earned Chinook Pass. Nothing on the route asks for a skill she
hasn't already practiced on an easier road earlier in the week. **Her confidence sets the pace
throughout** — the curriculum only "works" if no one pushes her past the day's lesson.

## What makes these roads good to *ride* (Ruslan's lens)

- **Hood Canal (WA-106):** the quiet, flowing shoreline warm-up — the kind of road you ride slow on purpose.
- **Three Capes Loop:** the coast's best low-speed cornering, lighthouse-to-dune, away from the highway traffic.
- **Alsea River road (OR-34):** shaded, river-following forest sweepers — the trip's first "rider's road" inland.
- **Spirit Lake Hwy (SR-504):** genuinely one of WA's best paved sweeper roads, climbing into a blast zone — a viewpoint-to-viewpoint joy.
- **Stevens Canyon Rd & Chinook Pass (SR-410):** the payoff — alpine, dramatic, the mountain in the windscreen, switchbacks to the top of the trip. The road *is* the reward.

## PNW motorcycle culture (context for the family)

- **A big, year-round riding region.** The Cascades and the coast make the Pacific Northwest one of the best touring areas in the U.S., with a strong, friendly rider community despite the wet reputation (locals just gear up for rain).
- **The local skill scene:** the region is home to respected rider-training names — **Bret Tkacs** (PSSOR / off-road & ADV coaching, who Ruslan rode Morocco with) is PNW-based — a nice thread, since this trip is in part Galiya's "real-world graduate course" after the endorsement class.
- **Pass season & the ritual of "the passes opening."** PNW riders track WSDOT pass reports each spring; **Chinook Pass reopening for the season** is a genuine local riding milestone — which makes it a fitting graduation road. (Confirm it's open before Day 7 — see scenic-routes.md.)
- **Iconic regional rides beyond this loop** (for future tours): the **North Cascades Hwy (SR-20)**, **Mount Baker Hwy (SR-542)**, the **Olympic Peninsula loop (US-101)**, the **Columbia River Gorge / Historic Hwy** and Oregon's **McKenzie Pass (OR-242)** and the full Oregon Coast. Good "next trip" goals once Galiya has this first tour under her belt.
- **The machines-and-engines side trips** (Tillamook Air Museum, Maritime Museum, optional Spruce Goose / LeMay) are catalogued in [`../research/motorcycle-museums.md`](../research/motorcycle-museums.md).
